# Ranking & Abstention (Selective Prediction) for Id./supra Antecedent Attribution

**Date:** 2026-06-02
**Query:** Learning-to-rank and selective-prediction/abstention foundations for a legal short-form-citation (Id./supra) antecedent attributor — both a future learned model and abstention as a *present* design principle in the deterministic resolver.
**Depth:** deep

## Summary

The single most actionable finding: **abstention is a first-class design primitive, not an ML-only feature — adopt it in the deterministic resolver now.** The reject-option literature (Chow 1970 onward) shows that the right policy when confidence is low is to *decline*, and that an optimal abstainer refuses whenever the posterior risk of being wrong exceeds the cost of a deferral. In our high-stakes setting a confident-wrong antecedent (silently mis-attributing a quote to Corsello instead of Hogue) is strictly worse than a "flag for review," so a deferral-when-ambiguous gate is the correct posture even before any learning. Second, the 1-of-N antecedent choice is exactly the **mention-ranking** formulation from neural coreference (Lee et al. 2017): score each candidate antecedent against the anaphor, include an explicit *dummy / null* candidate, and pick the argmax — a framing that drops cleanly onto Id./supra. Third, for a learned ranker, **LambdaMART** (Burges 2010) over hand-designed features (scope depth, recency, reporter/jurisdiction match, party-name overlap, intervening-cite count) is the pragmatic choice — feature-based, small-data-friendly, interpretable. Fourth, **conformal prediction** (Vovk/Gammerman/Shafer 2005; Angelopoulos & Bates 2021) gives a *distribution-free, finite-sample* coverage guarantee under only **exchangeability**, and its set-valued output maps directly onto an abstention rule: predict only on **singleton** prediction sets, **flag for review** on empty (novelty) or ≥2-label (ambiguity) sets (Szabadváry et al. 2025). This is the statistical backbone for "calibrated fail-closed deferral."

## Key Findings

### 1. The reject option is old, well-founded, and matches our risk asymmetry (Chow 1970)
- Selective prediction / classification-with-a-reject-option has existed ~60 years (Geifman & El-Yaniv 2017 open with exactly this framing). **Chow (1970)** derived the optimal error–reject trade-off: abstain whenever the conditional expected risk of mis-classification exceeds the reject cost; the Bayes-optimal abstainer rejects exactly in that region.
- Implication for us: the *parameter* that governs deferral is the **relative cost of a wrong attribution vs. a deferral**. In legal citation checking those costs are wildly asymmetric (a wrong "Id." target propagates a wrong quote/verdict; a deferral just routes to a human). Chow's rule says: with that asymmetry, set the abstention threshold aggressively — defer readily.

### 2. 1-of-N antecedent selection IS mention-ranking (Lee et al. 2017, EMNLP)
- The coreference task "which earlier mention does this one refer to?" is, for the *n*-th mention, an *n*-way choice over the *n−1* preceding mentions **plus a dummy** "refers to nothing new" option (confirmed in multiple secondary summaries; the dummy is the anaphoricity escape hatch).
- Verified formulation (Lee et al. 2017, fetched from the ACL/arXiv source): each span *i* gets a distribution over candidate antecedents *j* via a **softmax over pairwise scores** `s(i,j)`, with a **dummy antecedent ε scored `s(i,ε)=0`** so a non-anaphoric span concentrates mass on ε. The pairwise score decomposes as a *mention* score for *i*, a mention score for *j*, and an **antecedent-compatibility** term: `s(i,j) = s_m(i) + s_m(j) + s_a(i,j)`. Training maximizes the **marginal log-likelihood** of the *set* of gold antecedents: `log Σ_{j∈GOLD(i)} P(j)` — i.e. it does not require a single labeled antecedent, only that the chosen one be *some* correct one.
- The model mixes learned span representations with **explicit features**: a same-speaker indicator, genre metadata, **span-distance buckets**, and span length. This is the template for our feature set.
- The *dummy-antecedent ↔ abstention* link is direct: a high-scoring dummy = "no good antecedent." We can reuse that exact mechanism so the ranker can *natively* say "none of the candidates is a confident match → flag."

### 3. LambdaMART is the pragmatic learned-ranker engine (Burges 2010, MSR-TR-2010-82)
- **RankNet**: pairwise model, `P(i ranked above j) = σ(s_i − s_j)`, trained with cross-entropy on score *differences* via gradient descent. Learns from *relative* judgments (which antecedent is more correct) — ideal when we have per-document "the right antecedent is X" labels but no absolute scores.
- **LambdaRank's key trick**: you don't need to differentiate the (non-differentiable) ranking metric. You *specify the gradient* directly: `λ_ij = −σ/(1+e^{σ(s_i−s_j)}) · |ΔZ_ij|`, where **`|ΔZ_ij|` is the change in an IR metric (e.g. NDCG) caused by swapping i and j**. This focuses learning on swaps that matter for the metric.
- **LambdaMART** = LambdaRank's lambdas inside **gradient-boosted regression trees (MART)**. Feature-based, robust on small/medium tabular data, no GPU, and **interpretable feature importances** — which matters for a legal product (you can explain *why* an antecedent was chosen).
- For us, ranking metric collapses to top-1 accuracy (we need *the* antecedent), so the metric weighting simplifies, but the pairwise/feature machinery still applies.

### 4. Conformal prediction = the calibrated, distribution-free abstention backbone (Vovk et al. 2005; Angelopoulos & Bates 2021)
- **What it provides** (verified from Angelopoulos & Bates 2021, fetched): split/inductive conformal computes nonconformity scores `s_i` on a held-out **calibration set** of size *n*, sets the threshold `q̂ = Quantile(s_1..s_n; ⌈(n+1)(1−α)⌉/n)`, and forms the prediction set `C(x) = { y : s(x,y) ≤ q̂ }`. The **finite-sample marginal coverage theorem** is two-sided:
  `1 − α ≤ P(Y_test ∈ C(X_test)) ≤ 1 − α + 1/(n+1)`.
- **The only assumption is exchangeability** of calibration + test points — strictly *weaker* than i.i.d.: the joint distribution is invariant under permutation of the sequence. No distributional/parametric assumptions on the data or the underlying model; it wraps *any* scorer (NN, SVM, kNN, or our heuristic).
- **Critical caveat**: coverage is **marginal** (averaged over draws), *not conditional* on a given input x. `P(Y∈C(X) | X=x) ≥ 1−α` is a much stronger property that split-conformal does *not* deliver. So "95% coverage" is a population-average promise, not a per-citation promise — relevant when we report guarantees to lawyers.
- **Reject-option mapping** (Szabadváry et al. 2025, *Machine Learning with Applications*, arXiv:2506.21802): in (binary/multiclass) classification, a conformal predictor outputs sets of size 0, 1, or ≥2. **Accept only singletons**; treat **empty set = novelty rejection** (nothing fits — structure unrecoverable / no candidate matches) and **≥2 labels = ambiguity rejection** (multiple plausible antecedents). This yields a classifier-with-reject whose *error rate on accepted (singleton) predictions* is controlled. This is *exactly* our abstain logic: 1 confident antecedent → attribute; 0 or many → flag.

### 5. Selective classification gives a "dial" with a guaranteed-risk bound (Geifman & El-Yaniv 2017, NIPS; SelectiveNet, ICML 2019)
- Verified formal apparatus (Geifman & El-Yaniv 2017, read from PDF): a **selective classifier is a pair (f, g)** with predictor `f:X→Y` and **selection/gating function `g:X→{0,1}`**; output is `f(x)` if `g(x)=1`, else **"don't know"** (abstain). Define **coverage** `φ(f,g)=E[g(x)]` and **selective risk** `R(f,g)=E[ℓ(f(x),y)·g(x)] / φ(f,g)` (risk computed *only over accepted points*). The whole behavior is summarized by the **risk–coverage curve**.
- **SGR (Selection with Guaranteed Risk)**: given a trained `f`, a target risk `r*`, and confidence `δ`, binary-search a confidence threshold so the resulting selective risk satisfies `Pr{R(f,g) > r*} < δ` (uses a tight numerical generalization bound, Gascuel-Caraux). Empirically they hit, e.g., 1% error at ~78% coverage on CIFAR-10. The lesson: *you can pick the error rate you can live with and pay for it in coverage.*
- The simplest **confidence-rate function** they use is **softmax response (SR)**: `κ(x)=max_j f(x|j)` — just the top class probability. The paper notes the score only needs to **rank** correctly (be monotone in true loss), not be a calibrated probability — so even a crude margin can drive a good reject rule.
- **SelectiveNet (ICML 2019)** trains `f` and `g` *jointly, end-to-end* for a target coverage, beating threshold-on-pretrained-network. Relevant only once we have a learned model; not needed for the deterministic phase.

### 6. Feature-based coref rankers work with shallow surface features (Durrett & Klein 2013, EMNLP)
- Their Berkeley system places "a distribution over antecedence choices with a log-linear model" using "a small number of homogeneous feature templates examining shallow properties of mentions" — and is strong on **syntax and discourse** while *weak on semantics*. This validates a small, surface-feature ranker for antecedent choice (our scope/recency/reporter/party features are exactly such shallow, discourse-structural signals) and warns that semantic features add little — keep the feature set lean and structural.

## How This Applies to Id./supra Attribution

**The canonical bug, restated in this vocabulary.** Given
`Hogue v. State, 123 U.S. 1 (2020) (quoting Corsello v. Verizon, 456 N.Y. 2 (2010)).`
a following `Id.` should resolve to **Hogue** (outer, sibling-scope, in discourse focus). Recency picks **Corsello** because it is textually nearest — but Corsello lives *inside* Hogue's `(quoting …)` parenthetical, i.e. in an **inner scope** that should be **invisible** to an outer-scope anaphor.

**Mapping onto the 5-stage pipeline:**

- **Stages 1–2 (PARSE + SCOPE) are a hard candidate filter, upstream of any ranking/abstention.** Use the bracket-stack parser to compute each candidate's **scope depth**, then *remove* parenthetical-internal candidates from the candidate set for an outer `Id.` (Corsello is filtered out entirely). This is the analog of coreference's hard constraints. It is also why **eyecite's resolver fails our bug**: eyecite resolves Id./supra "using only its immanent knowledge about each citation's textual representation" (verified from its README) — a flat, text-proximity heuristic with **no documented nested-citation, parenthetical, or abstention handling**. Recency is the field-standard baseline precisely because nobody models scope. Our edge is doing Stage 2 at all.

- **Stage 3 (SALIENCE) = mention-ranking over the *surviving* sibling-scope candidates.** Frame it exactly as Lee et al. 2017: the `Id.` is the "anaphor"; each surviving full citation is a candidate antecedent; score `s(Id., cand)`; argmax wins. **Include a dummy/null candidate** (`s=0`) — its winning is the native "no good antecedent" signal, the abstention escape hatch baked into the ranker. Start deterministic (a weighted score, scope-depth-aware), and the *same candidate-set + feature-vector seam* later feeds a LambdaMART model with zero pipeline change.

- **Stage 4 (ABSTAIN) is the load-bearing finding — and it should govern the deterministic resolver NOW.** Two converging arguments:
  1. **Risk asymmetry (Chow 1970).** A confident-wrong attribution corrupts downstream quote-verification and verdicts and is hard for a reviewer to catch; a deferral is cheap and self-announcing. With cost(wrong) ≫ cost(defer), the optimal rule abstains readily. **Never silently fall back to recency** — that is the maximally-confident-wrong behavior the literature says to avoid.
  2. **Calibrated, fail-closed deferral via conformal (Szabadváry et al. 2025 / Vovk 2005).** Even without ML, we can wrap the deterministic scorer: build a small **calibration set** of hand-labeled Id./supra→antecedent cases, compute nonconformity scores (e.g. `1 − normalized_salience` of the chosen candidate, or margin to runner-up), and apply the conformal threshold. Then **attribute only on a singleton conformal set; flag on empty (no candidate clears threshold → "structure unrecoverable / no match") or on ≥2 (genuine ambiguity, e.g. two equally-recent sibling citations)**. This buys a *distribution-free, finite-sample* statement like "on accepted attributions our error is ≤ α" — defensible to lawyers — under just **exchangeability** of our labeled corpus.
  - Concretely, abstain whenever: (a) the stack parser could not recover bracket structure for the relevant region (dirty PDF); (b) after scoping, **0** sibling-scope candidates remain; (c) the salience **margin** between top-1 and top-2 is below the conformal threshold; (d) the dummy/null candidate ranks at or near the top.

- **Stage 5 (PROVENANCE)** is orthogonal to ranking/abstention but benefits: once Hogue is the confident antecedent and Corsello is recorded as the *inner quoted* source, you can emit the chain "quoted in Hogue, originally Corsello" — and the scope tree built in Stages 1–2 is exactly the data structure for it.

**Feature design for the LambdaMART attributor** (per-candidate feature vector, anaphor × candidate):
- **scope_depth_delta** — difference in bracket-nesting depth between anaphor and candidate (the *killer* feature for our bug; an inner-scope candidate should be hard-filtered, but as a soft feature it down-weights deep candidates). *Highest expected importance.*
- **recency_rank / intervening_cite_count** — how many citations sit between them (the old heuristic, demoted to one feature among many).
- **reporter_match / jurisdiction_match** — same reporter series or court (an `Id.` rarely jumps jurisdiction silently).
- **party_name_overlap** — token/Jaccard overlap of case names (helps `supra` especially, which is name-anchored: "Bush, supra").
- **citation_form_compatibility** — is the short form even *type-compatible* with the candidate (e.g. `supra` favors secondary authorities; `Id.` favors the immediately-preceding *accessible* authority).
- **same_sentence / same_footnote / same_block indicators** — discourse-structural proximity (Durrett & Klein: syntax/discourse features carry the weight).
- **pincite_consistency** — does the `Id.` pincite page fall within the candidate's reporter range.
Keep it lean and structural; Durrett & Klein found shallow semantic features add little.

## Trade-offs & Alternatives

- **Deterministic-scope-filter vs. learned ranker, NOW.** The hard scope filter (Stage 2) fixes the canonical bug *deterministically* and is auditable. A learned LambdaMART ranker only earns its keep for the *soft* sibling-scope tie-breaks (Stage 3) and needs labeled data. **Sequencing: ship Stages 1–2 + abstention (Stage 4) first; defer the learned ranker.** The mention-ranking *frame* should still shape the deterministic scorer so the ML drop-in is seam-compatible.
- **Conformal coverage is marginal, not conditional.** We can promise a population-average error rate on accepted attributions, *not* a per-citation guarantee. Do not over-claim. Mondrian/class-conditional conformal can recover per-group guarantees (e.g. per-citation-type) at the cost of more calibration data.
- **Exchangeability can break.** If the brief corpus drifts (new jurisdictions, new PDF-extraction pipeline changing the score distribution), conformal validity degrades. Mitigation: periodic re-calibration; treat the calibration set as a maintained asset.
- **LambdaMART vs. softmax/Plackett-Luce listwise vs. neural span model.** LambdaMART: best for small tabular feature sets, interpretable, CPU. A softmax-cross-entropy listwise ranker (ListNet-style / the Lee-et-al softmax) is simpler to wire to a NN and gives calibrated-ish probabilities feeding conformal directly. A full neural span model (Lee et al.) is overkill — we already *have* the citation spans from eyecite; we don't need end-to-end mention detection.
- **Confidence source for abstention.** Softmax response (SR) is the cheapest confidence-rate function and Geifman & El-Yaniv show it suffices (only needs to rank-correlate with loss). But for a *deterministic* resolver we have no softmax — use **margin-to-runner-up** or a **calibrated heuristic score** as the nonconformity measure. SelectiveNet's jointly-trained gate is strictly a future option.
- **Reject-option (Chow/Geifman) vs. learning-to-defer (Madras et al. 2018).** Plain reject = abstain to *a* human. Learning-to-defer additionally models *which* downstream reviewer and their accuracy/bias. Given we already route to human review, plain reject is the right scope now; learning-to-defer is a later refinement if reviewer modeling ever matters.

## Open Questions

1. **What α (target error on accepted attributions) is acceptable to the product/legal side?** This sets the conformal threshold and the coverage we sacrifice. Needs a product decision, informed by a risk–coverage curve on our own data.
2. **How big a labeled calibration set do we need?** Conformal slack is `1/(n+1)`; a few hundred labeled Id./supra→antecedent cases gives a usable guarantee, but the *nested-parenthetical* subpopulation (our bug) may be rare — may need targeted labeling so the calibration set is exchangeable with production.
3. **Is the deterministic salience score monotone enough in true correctness to be a good nonconformity measure?** If not, conformal still gives *valid* coverage but *wide* (low-coverage) sets — we'd abstain too much. Worth an offline check.
4. **Multi-label gold antecedents?** Lee et al. marginalize over a *set* of correct antecedents. Could a single `Id.` legitimately have >1 acceptable target (e.g. a string cite)? If so, our "≥2 ⇒ abstain" rule may over-defer; revisit.
5. **Where exactly is the candidate-set/feature seam in the current resolver code?** The mention-ranking frame is only free-to-adopt-later if Stage 3 is built as "score a candidate list," not as inline recency picking. (Code-side question for the resolver implementer.)
