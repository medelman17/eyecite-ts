# Scope, Binding & Lexical Scoping as the Mechanism for Id./supra Attribution

**Date:** 2026-06-02
**Query:** Is the "a cite inside `(quoting …)` is invisible to an outer `Id.`" rule better modeled by GB/c-command (structural dominance) or by programming-language lexical scoping (name accessibility across nested blocks)? Which is more accurate and more implementable, how do symbol-table algorithms decide cross-scope visibility, and do Binding Principles A/B/C actually transfer to citation anaphora?
**Depth:** deep

## Summary

**Lexical scoping / symbol-table name resolution is the correct mechanism; c-command is a rhetorical analogy and should not be implemented literally.** The "inner cite invisible to outer `Id.`" rule is the *exact* behavior a block-structured symbol table already gives you for free: a name (citation) declared in a nested scope (a `(quoting …)` parenthetical) is reachable only from inside that scope and its children, never from an enclosing scope — because, as compiler texts put it, "each scope stores a pointer to its parent, but not vice-versa" (Sarkar, *CMPT 379*). The canonical bug is confirmed at the code level in the de-facto industry baseline, eyecite: its `_resolve_id_citation` returns `last_resolution` (the most recently *seen* full cite in document order) with, verbatim, "NO awareness of parenthetical nesting, brackets, or scope" — so a `FullCitation` buried inside a parenthetical is pushed onto resolution history identically to a top-level one, and the following `Id.` binds to it. Critically, even *modern computational linguistics has abandoned c-command* for binding: the leading neuro-symbolic formalization (Branco 2021) defines binding over **o-command** — an order on the obliqueness hierarchy of grammatical functions (HPSG), not tree dominance — which means the GB machinery the analogy invokes is not even how working binding-theory systems are built. Take-away for the resolver: implement Stage-2 SCOPE as a hard symbol-table visibility filter over a stack-parsed bracket tree; borrow from Binding Theory only the *taxonomy* (anaphor vs. pronominal locality, Principle C's "antecedent can't be inside a domain it should dominate"), not its tree-configurational apparatus.

## Key Findings

### 1. c-command originates with Reinhart (1976), is defined over *parse-tree nodes*, and is the wrong object for typographic nesting
- The term and standard definition are **Tanya Reinhart's 1976 MIT dissertation**, not Chomsky's: "α c-commands β iff every branching node dominating α dominates β." The common variant is the *first-branching-node* rule: "the first (i.e. lowest) branching node that dominates N1 also dominates N2." (Reinhart 1976, via Wikipedia *C-command*.) **This matters for attribution:** the proposed pipeline credits c-command to "Chomsky (1981)," but the relation is Reinhart's and Chomsky's *Lectures on Government and Binding* (Foris, 1981) *uses* it as a primitive in stating the Binding Principles.
- c-command is explicitly **a relation over nodes in a syntactic constituent tree**: "A node in a parse tree c-commands its sister node and all of its sister's descendants." A `(quoting …)` parenthetical is *typographic/document* nesting, not a sentence constituent parse — there is no licensed syntactic tree in which "Hogue" and "Corsello" stand in a sister/dominance relation. Applying c-command requires first inventing a parse the source does not provide. (See Pitfalls.)

### 2. Binding Theory's three principles (Chomsky 1981) — and what each actually says
Verified verbatim (Wikipedia *Government and binding theory*; corroborated by Branco 2021 and Pressbooks *The Science of Syntax*):
- **Principle A** — "an anaphor (reflexive or reciprocal …) must be bound in its governing category (roughly, the clause)." (Locality: must find a *near* binder.)
- **Principle B** — "a pronoun must be free (i.e., not bound) within its governing category." (Anti-locality: binder must be *outside* the local domain.)
- **Principle C** — "an R-expression must be free … R-expressions … independently refer."
- Binding itself = **coindexation + c-command**: "An element α binds an element β iff α c-commands β, and α and β corefer." So binding is structural dominance *plus* coreference. The "governing category / binding domain" is "essentially the minimal clause or complex NP" — a *locality* envelope.

### 3. Modern computational binding theory replaces c-command with o-command (obliqueness), confirming c-command is dispensable
This is the strongest evidence against the c-command model. Branco (2021), *Anaphoric Binding: an integrated overview* (arXiv:2103.06924, cs.CL), the integrated neuro-symbolic formalization, defines **four** constraints (A, **Z** [long-distance reflexive], B, C) entirely over **o-command**, not c-command:
- "x o-binds y iff x o-commands y and x and y are coindexed."
- "Y o-commands Z just in case either Y is less oblique than Z; or Y o-commands some X that subcategorises for Z; or Y o-commands some X that is a projection of Z." — an order on the **obliqueness hierarchy of grammatical functions** (Subject < Direct Object < Indirect Object …), the HPSG `ARG-ST` ordering of Pollard & Sag (1987/1994).
- Principle C as Branco states it: **"A non-pronoun must be o-free"** — i.e. a referring expression must not be o-bound by anything. Example: `…[Lee's friend] thinks [[Max's brother] likes the boy*]` — *the boy* cannot corefer with the c-/o-commanding `Max's brother`.
**Implication:** even within linguistics, "binding" in deployable systems is a *containment/ordering* relation among argument-structure positions, not raw tree geometry or text proximity. The thing that transfers to citations is "an outer reference cannot reach into a more-embedded/oblique position to find its antecedent" — which is *also* exactly the lexical-scoping visibility rule. c-command is one (superseded) implementation of that intuition.

### 4. Lexical (static) scoping + symbol tables give the visibility rule directly
From Sarkar, *Scoping and Symbol Tables* (CMPT 379, SFU) and the Wisconsin *CS 536* symbol-table lecture:
- **Static/lexical scope** = "the scope of a variable is determined by the program's textual (lexical) structure" — fixed by *where it is written*, resolved at compile time. The citation analogue: a parenthetical's contents are scoped by *where the brackets sit in the document*, decidable statically from the parse.
- **Symbol table = a stack** with four primitives: "**Push scope** (enter), **Pop scope** (leave, discarding all declarations), **Insert symbol** (add to current scope), **Lookup symbol** (find a descriptor)." Map: open-paren ⇒ Push; close-paren ⇒ Pop (the inner full cite's declaration is discarded as you exit); a full citation ⇒ Insert into the current scope; an `Id.`/`supra` ⇒ Lookup.
- **The visibility asymmetry — the whole ballgame:** "Treat the symbol table as a linked structure of scopes. **Each scope stores a pointer to its parent, but not vice-versa.** From any point … the symbol table appears to be a stack." Lookup searches the *current* scope, then walks *parent pointers outward*; it can never descend into a sibling or child scope. Therefore **an outer `Id.` literally cannot see a citation declared inside a parenthetical** — not by penalty, but by construction. Conversely an `Id.` *inside* the parenthetical correctly sees the inner cite first, then the outer ones.
- **Most-closely-nested / shadowing:** inner declarations hide outer ones of the same name ("introduction of new variables … may hide older variables"). Citation analogue: an `Id.` inside a `(quoting Weinreb…)` that itself follows the outer cite resolves to Weinreb, not the outer case — correct.
- **Unresolved = abstain:** when Lookup finds the name in *no* enclosing scope, compilers raise an "undeclared name" error rather than guess. This is precisely Stage-4 ABSTAIN: structure recovered, no in-scope antecedent ⇒ FLAG FOR REVIEW, never silently fall back to recency.

### 5. The baseline (eyecite) is pure recency, confirmed in source — this *is* the bug
- `_resolve_id_citation(id_citation, last_resolution, resolutions)` "returns the immediately preceding resolved citation's resource" — `return last_resolution`. The main loop iterates **in document order** and updates `last_resolution = resolution` for every citation, "regardless of contextual boundaries."
- For nesting specifically (verbatim from the source read): "**There is NO awareness of parenthetical nesting, brackets, or scope.** … A `FullCitation` found inside a parenthetical is added to `resolved_full_cites` and becomes `last_resolution` exactly like any other citation." This is the mechanical cause of the Hogue/Corsello bug: Corsello is Inserted into history while *inside* Hogue's parenthetical, then the following `Id.` Lookups → `last_resolution` = Corsello.
- eyecite's own escape hatch is exactly our redesign surface: it lets callers "pass custom `resolve_id_citation()` / `resolve_supra_citation()` …" — i.e. the scope-aware resolver plugs in here without forking extraction. (`supra` already does *antecedent-text* matching — `_filter_by_matching_antecedent` against plaintiff/defendant, returning a resource "only if exactly one match exists," else None — a partial, accidental abstain.)

### 6. Salience (Stage 3) has its own canonical theory: Centering
For ranking *remaining in-scope* candidates, the established model is **Centering Theory** — Grosz, Joshi & Weinstein (1995), "Centering: A Framework for Modeling the Local Coherence of Discourse," *Computational Linguistics* 21(2):203–225 (ACL Anthology J95-2003). It ranks forward-looking centers (Cf) by grammatical prominence (**Subject > Object > others**) to predict the preferred antecedent of a following pronoun. Citation analogue: among sibling-scope candidates, the *grammatically/discourse-focal* cite (the case the sentence is *about* — typically the outer "holding" cite, the textual subject) outranks one merely mentioned in a parenthetical aside. Note the convergence with Branco's obliqueness hierarchy (Subject = least oblique = highest binder): **two independent traditions both rank by grammatical-function prominence, not text distance.**

## How This Applies to Id./supra Attribution

Mapping the staged pipeline onto the verified mechanisms:

| Pipeline stage | Mechanism (with source) | Concrete rule for the resolver |
|---|---|---|
| **1. PARSE (stack)** | Bracket matching = balanced-parens via a stack (classic; arXiv:2101.09699 treats it as the textbook stack problem). | Tokenize `(` `)` `[` `]`; push/pop to build a **scope tree** of the document. Dirty-PDF tolerance = error-recovery (unmatched close ⇒ pop-to-root or open a repair scope), *not* a linear paren counter. |
| **2. SCOPE (hard filter)** | Symbol-table visibility: "each scope stores a pointer to its parent, but not vice-versa" (Sarkar). Mirrors Branco's "must be o-free outside its domain." | For an `Id.`/`supra` at node N, **Lookup walks N's scope then parent-pointers outward only.** Citations in sibling/child scopes are *invisible*. This deletes Corsello from Hogue's outer `Id.` candidate set by construction. **HARD filter, not a penalty.** |
| **3. SALIENCE (soft rank)** | Centering Cf-ranking by grammatical prominence (Grosz/Joshi/Weinstein 1995); converges with Branco obliqueness (Subject highest). | Among the surviving sibling-scope cites, prefer the discourse-focal/"subject" cite (outer holding cite) over incidental mentions. Recency becomes one weak feature *within a scope*, never a cross-scope override. |
| **4. ABSTAIN** | Compiler "undeclared name" behavior: no in-scope binding ⇒ error, not a guess (Sarkar/CS536). | If the scope tree can't be recovered (unbalanced/dirty) **or** Lookup yields zero / ≥2 equally-salient in-scope candidates ⇒ FLAG FOR REVIEW. Never fall back to `last_resolution`. (eyecite's `supra` "exactly one match else None" is the right instinct, generalized.) |
| **5. PROVENANCE** | Scope chain = the parent-pointer path. | Record the Lookup's walked chain to reconstruct "language quoted in Hogue, originally from Weinreb…" for free — it's the path from the `Id.`'s scope up to the resolved declaration. |

**The canonical bug, resolved:** In `"Hogue …(quoting Corsello…)." Id.`, the parser opens a child scope at `(quoting`, Inserts Corsello *there*, and Pops it at `)`. Hogue is Inserted in the root scope. The trailing `Id.` lives in the root scope; Lookup searches root (and outward), where only Hogue is visible — Corsello's declaration was discarded on Pop. **Hogue wins, deterministically, with no salience heuristic even needed.** Salience only arbitrates the harder case of *two sibling-scope* cites.

**Where to build it:** behind eyecite's `resolve_id_citation` / `resolve_supra_citation` injection points — keep eyecite's extraction, replace the resolver with a scope-tree Lookup. (Repo uses eyecite-ts; same resolution seam.)

## Trade-offs & Alternatives

- **Lexical scoping vs. c-command — verdict:** Lexical scoping wins on *both* axes the task demands.
  - *Accuracy:* The phenomenon is bracket/document nesting (a real, typed structure in the text), which is exactly what scopes model. c-command needs a *syntactic constituent tree* that the source doesn't contain and that nested cites don't actually inhabit — and modern binding theory itself (Branco 2021) has replaced c-command with o-command, so even the linguistic pedigree points away from it.
  - *Implementability:* Scoping is a ~50-line stack + parent-pointer lookup with a 60-year-deployed-in-every-compiler track record and a built-in abstain (undeclared-name). c-command would require building/validating parse trees over noisy legal prose — high cost, low robustness.
  - **Keep from Binding Theory:** the *vocabulary and locality taxonomy* — anaphor (must bind locally, like `Id.` = nearest in-scope) vs. pronominal (binds non-locally, looser, like `supra` reaching back across the document); and Principle C's insight ("a referring expression can't be bound from inside a position it outranks") as a sanity check that an outer authority shouldn't be "captured" by an embedded one. These are conceptual scaffolding, not code.

- **Stack parser vs. regex/linear paren counter:** A depth counter can detect *depth* but not *identity/scope membership* and shatters on dirty input (an unmatched `)` from OCR desyncs every subsequent depth). A stack with explicit error-recovery degrades gracefully and feeds Stage-4 abstain. Strongly prefer the stack.

- **Hard scope filter vs. soft proximity penalty:** A soft penalty ("downweight cites inside parentheticals") will still occasionally let a strongly-recent inner cite win — i.e. it *reintroduces the bug probabilistically*. Scope must be a **hard candidate filter** (Stage 2), with salience (Stage 3) operating only on survivors. This is the single most important design commitment.

- **What scoping does NOT solve:** intra-scope ambiguity (two sibling cites, same scope) and dirty structure both still need Stages 3–4. Scoping converts an *unbounded* search (all preceding text) into a *bounded, structurally-licensed* one — it shrinks and de-biases the candidate set; it doesn't pick the winner inside a scope.

- **Convergent vs. divergent sources:** High-confidence convergence — Sarkar, CS536, PEP 227, and ScienceDirect all agree on the outward-only nested-scope lookup with parent (not child) visibility; eyecite source + docs agree it's bug-for-bug recency. Productive divergence — c-command (Reinhart 1976 / GB tree-configurational) vs. o-command (Branco 2021 / HPSG obliqueness) is a *genuine theoretical split* in how to formalize binding; we side with the relational/functional camp because it both matches the data better and is what gets implemented.

## Open Questions

1. **Bracket recovery on real-world input.** How often is `(quoting …)` *not* delimited by clean parens after PDF→markdown (em-dash sets, dropped closes, smart-quote noise)? The whole edifice rests on recoverable structure; needs a measurement pass on the corpus before committing the hard filter. (Cross-ref the parsing-track report and "Hidden Structure," arXiv:2505.12837, on formatting-loss in legal docs.)
2. **`Id.` vs. `supra` as anaphor vs. pronominal.** Is `Id.` strictly *most-closely-nested* (Principle-A-like locality) while `supra` legitimately reaches *across* sibling/closed scopes by name (Principle-B/pronominal non-locality)? If so they need *different* scope-traversal rules, not one resolver. (eyecite already special-cases `supra` via antecedent text — partial evidence yes.)
3. **Block-vs-inline scopes.** Do all legal nesting devices create scopes, or only *quoting/citing* parentheticals? An *explanatory* parenthetical `(holding that…)` with no embedded cite is an empty scope (harmless); but does a string-cite `(citing A; B; C)` create one scope with three siblings, or three peer scopes? Affects salience ranking.
4. **Does Centering's Subject-first ranking actually predict the outer cite?** Plausible and convergent with obliqueness, but untested on citations. Needs a labeled eval before salience weights are set; until then, prefer abstain over a guessed rank.
5. **Quantitative lift.** What share of real `Id.`/supra misattributions are *scope* errors (fixed hard by Stage 2) vs. *intra-scope salience* errors (needing Stage 3)? Determines where to invest.
